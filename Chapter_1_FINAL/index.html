UNDERSTANDING A HYDROGEN ATOM

Before we begin our journey looking at how semiconductor devices work, we need to understand the inner workings of atoms. The Hydrogen Atom, due to its simple structure, has been extensively studied, and provides a good starting point to understand the fundamentals, before we move onto semiconductors such as Silicon.

The Subatomic Particles

Each atom of any element is composed of certain subatomic particles. These subatomic particles are the electron, the proton and the neutron.

An electron is a negative charge carrier, with negligible mass. Each element has as many electrons as its atomic number. A proton is a positive charge carrier, and contributes to the mass of the atom. Together, these two subatomic particles ensure that the atom is neutral, or has a net charge of 0.

A hydrogen atom has 1 proton and 1 electron, but no neutron.

The Classical Model

The Classical Model is a planetary model in which the negatively charged electrons orbit a small, positively charged nucleus similar to the planets orbiting the sun.

In the case of the Hydrogen atom, the nucleus contains only the proton, and the elctron revolves around it due to Coulomb forces.

The Classical Model contains some errors, but it is important because it describes most of the accepted features of atomic theory without all of the high-level math of the modern version.

The Quantum Model

As our understanding became clearer, we developed ways of predicting the location of these subatomic particles in space.

We found the behaviour of protons and neutrons to mimic the Classical Model - they always stayed fixed inside the nucleus.

The electron, however, behaved differently, and was located at a different location everytime.

Exploring the Distribution

Scientists plotted more than a million of these predictions along space. This data of predicted locations was used to draw correlations between the location of electrons and the distance to the nucleus.

As you can see in the adjoining visualization, most of the predictions they made were around the nucleus. Moreover, as the distance from the nucleus increased, the density of predictions became sparser.

Zooming Out

As we zoom out, we can see that even though some predictions are farther from the nucleus, most of the times the electron is close to the nucleus.

They found that the highest number of predictoins were at the Bohr Radius, which was also defined as the average position of all predictions.

The 2s Orbital

Upon repeating the experiment multiple times, scietists found a variety of different distributions that existed. Each of these different distributions is called an "orbital".

The 2s orbital has electrons farther from the nucleus, indicating they're more loosely bound than the previous 1s orbital electrons.

The 2px Orbital

The 2p orbital breaks the radial symmetry so far observed. Electrons were found to be closer along a particular axis, mimicing the shape of a dumbbell.

The adjoining visualization shows a 2px orbital, where the electrons are closer to the x-axis.

2py and 2pz

Similarly, electrons also exist in 2py and 2pz orbitals. These are identical to the 2px orbitals, other than their spatial orientation.

For the sake of simplicity

We now understand that an electron's position is not fixed, and it can exist anywhere. However, such a definition would not allow us to study its interaction with other charge carriers, which is necessary to solidify the concepts of semiconductor physics

Hence, for the sake of simplicity, in the following lessons, we will visualize the electron as being static. However, the visualization of the orbital of the electron should remind you, that it is not in fact so!
